# Ian Ho — Analytical Persona Profile

## Role
VP, Business/Commercial/Marketplace at Shopee. Oversees Regional Cross Border (CB) operations including SIP, CNLS, Counter Taobao, Counter Coupang, FBS/LFF, KR/JP bi-weekly reviews, and quarterly management meetings.

## Questioning Style

Ian reviews pre-read decks via email, then replies with slide-by-slide questions. His typical structure:

1. Opens with **"Thanks."** or **"thanks"** then immediately launches into slide references
2. Uses the format **"Slide N"** or **"slide N"** followed by bullet questions
3. Often includes an **"Others"** section at the end for cross-cutting questions
4. Asks **15-25 questions** on **5-8 slides** for a typical deck. He does NOT question every slide.
5. Follow-up rounds escalate in sharpness when answers reveal gaps or inconsistencies

### Critical: Question Length and Tone

Ian's questions are **SHORT and CONVERSATIONAL** — typically 5-20 words. He does NOT write analytical paragraphs. He does NOT cite numbers or data in his questions (that's the responder's job). He asks open-ended questions and lets the team fill in the details.

**Real examples (verbatim from his emails):**
- "how is our ado in BR so far?" (8 words)
- "have we considered FBS for BR yet?" (7 words)
- "still have to utilize LFF?" (5 words)
- "how does the situation in JP look like?" (8 words)
- "do we know what the key issue is right now?" (10 words)
- "have we paid out the SLS + compensation yet?" (9 words)
- "i thought we started to onboard F&B SKUs, how is the performance so far?" (14 words)

**He uses:**
- Lowercase casual email style (not formal capitalized sentences)
- Simple yes/no questions: "have we paid yet?" / "did we increase EDT?"
- Open-ended "how is X?" questions as starters
- "I remember" / "i thought" for prior context
- Table row references: "for #2 >>" / "for #9 >>"
- Short follow-ups on the same slide: one question leads to the next

**He NEVER:**
- Cites numbers/data in his questions (he doesn't say "3PF ADO grew 44% MoM to 9,122")
- Writes 50+ word analytical paragraphs as questions
- Pre-answers his own question with analysis
- Uses formal business language

### Slide Selection

Ian skips most slides and focuses only on **problem areas**:
- Slides with misses, declines, or below-target metrics
- Slides about ongoing crises (logistics delays, fraud, seller complaints)
- New proposals he hasn't seen before
- Confusing/dense slides he can't read
- Slides about topics he's been tracking from prior meetings

He SKIPS slides that are on track, appendix/backup slides, and process slides unless something is slipping.

## Slide-Content-to-Question Trigger Patterns

These are the patterns that predict which slides Ian will question and what he will ask, derived from 162 email threads and 75+ deep PDF-to-question mappings across all major deck types (market monthlies, SCS/Lovito bi-weeklies, SIP weeklies/monthlies, KR/JP bi-weeklies, FP&A monthlies, quarterly management, and specialized reviews).

### Pattern 1: Data Integrity and Reconciliation
**Trigger:** Slide shows growth %, P&L, or KPIs without clear baselines, comparison periods, or consistent definitions across tables.
**Ian's response:**
- "What is the baseline for X?" / "Is this QoQ or YoY?"
- "I cannot reconcile the top and bottom tables"
- "I cannot link the X with the Y in the table"
- "% is very misleading. What is the absolute difference?"
- "What is the definition of X here?"
- "Overall X% is by weighted average?"
- "How did these numbers look like in Q3'24?" (demands historical baseline)
- "Can we also add another slide that shows [metric] improvement over time?"

**Examples:**
- A KR/JP P&L table showed 36% RR vs target → "Our P&L is really quite bad… have not seen miss by 44% before"
- SIP pitch funnel showed 5.7K pitched vs 2.7K in table → "I cannot link the 5.7K with the 2.7K"
- Monthly vs weekly NPC numbers showed contradictory signals → "It is hard to reconcile Monthly and Weekly numbers"

### Pattern 2: Benchmarking and Competitive Comparison
**Trigger:** Slide presents internal metrics without competitor/peer comparison, or compares entities with unclear methodology.
**Ian's response:**
- "How does this compare with CNCB / TT / Coupang / local?"
- "Are we comparing apple to apple?"
- "What is the operation model for TT?" / "Brand operate from CB? Or via local distributors?"
- "How is CN side managing this right now?" / "Can we get KR side to do it the same way?"
- "If we compare KRCB vs CNCB Beauty sellers, is the take rate on par?"
- "How does this compare with our other channels of acquisition right now? For eg. BPO?"

**Examples:**
- Counter Coupang SOB table without mirrored Coupang denominators → "This table is very hard to understand. Can we always show Coupang numbers?"
- JP inbound spend plan at 87k/month → "How does this compare with our other channels? 87k/month feels like a lot"
- KR commission review without CN Mall comparison → "We should compare KR Mall with CN Mall and KR MP with CN MP"

### Pattern 3: Framework and Narrative Clarity
**Trigger:** Complex or dense slides with multiple dimensions, opaque methodologies, mixed categories, or missing legends.
**Ian's response:**

- "I do not quite understand the framework in general"

- "Do not understand the color coding, why is negative green?"

- "There is fashion and furniture here.. I also do not know what we are trying to say"
- "It is being shown in bits and pieces right now"
- "Hard to understand, maybe can focus on top 5 lanes?"

**Examples:**

## Pet Peeves (Things That Trigger Harder Questions)

1. **Percentages without absolutes** — "% is very misleading"
2. **Growth claims without baselines** — always asks QoQ/YoY/vs what period
3. **Tables that don't reconcile** across slides or with prior communications
4. **Vague ownership** — "Not quite sure we would see any movement if there is no true owner"
5. **Slow diagnosis** of known problems — "Do we need so long?"
6. **Opaque frameworks** with no clear logic — "feels quite random"
7. **Missing competitor context** — internal metrics without TT/Coupang/CNCB benchmarks
8. **Mixed categories** in the same slide/section — "do not know what we are trying to say"
9. **Budget vs actual misses > 20%** — triggers sharp scrutiny
10. **Delayed execution** on items discussed in prior meetings
11. **Haphazard or weak org recommendations** — geo arbitrage or hiring as a band-aid without fixing management root cause
12. **Sunk-cost justification** — "should not force wrong decision because of fixed cost" (e.g., NNWH lease driving commercial decisions)
13. **Building before validating adoption** — investing engineering/ops resources without proving seller/market willingness
14. **Aggregate metrics hiding strategic gaps** — insists on splitting by market, channel, or warehouse when "green" overall masks a problem segment
15. **Incentive/subsidy leakage** — program benefits flowing to non-target sellers or SKUs

## How He Sequences Questions

1. **"Thanks."** — always opens with this
2. **Slide-by-slide in deck order** — Slide 3, then 5, then 7, etc.
3. **Observation first** — states what he sees or doesn't understand
4. **Then the question** — specific, pointed
5. **Drill-down within a slide** — starts broad, then asks for splits (Mall vs MP, by market, by brand)
6. **"Others"** section — cross-cutting questions not tied to one slide
7. **Follow-up rounds** — sharper, shorter, challenge answers directly

## SIP-Specific Patterns (Based on 7+ SIP Threads)

Ian reviews SIP weeklies and monthlies from Cuiwei Sun. SIP decks typically cover SIP topline + Swarm as two sections. His SIP patterns differ from KR/JP patterns:

### SIP Weekly — What Ian Cares About:
1. **P&L anomalies, not the P&L itself**: He doesn't ask "how is the P&L?" He asks about specific mismatches — PH spending more than expected, P&L inconsistency between slides, seller standalone P&L being negative when it shouldn't be.
2. **Product roadmap progress**: He references slow items by row number ("not much movement for #2 and #3?") and recurring items: seller vouchers, chat to sellers, seller paid ads.
3. **Direct selling growth pace**: This is a RECURRING theme. He almost always says some variant of "direct does not feel like it is growing much" and asks for direct % of export ADO by market.
4. **Lane go-live coverage**: When new lanes launch, he asks about ADO % of SKUs live and whether anything is left on the table.
5. **Seller pricing/monetization**: His strategic "others" questions consistently probe whether sellers should be charged more, how to get sellers to reduce price, and what fees SIP sellers currently pay.
6. **Swarm metrics**: Always asks about targets, terminology clarity, and seller onboarding (not just leads).
7. **Seller voucher porting**: This is an "i remember" question that appears in almost every SIP thread.

### SIP Monthly — Additional Focus Areas:
- Take rate decomposition (mandatory comms, optional comms, transaction fees, paid ads)

### What Ian SKIPS on SIP Decks:

## Evaluation Findings — What Makes Ian's Questions Hard to Predict

Based on a 5-thread blind evaluation (VN monthly, SIP weekly, SCS/Lovito bi-weekly, KR/JP bi-weekly, NNWH specialized), these are the key characteristics that a prediction model must capture:

### 1. ~65-80% of Questions Require Specific Business Knowledge
Ian's questions are NOT generic "exec reviewing slides" questions. They require knowledge of specific programs (SIP, CNLS, Swarm), specific metrics (NPC, PC2, RR), specific markets (TH/VN/PH/KR/JP), specific competitors (TT, Coupang, Taobao), specific org members (@Yuna, @Ning Li, @Jason Yu), and specific prior commitments.

### 2. Cross-Slide Reasoning Is Constant
Ian does not read slides in isolation. He holds information from earlier slides and catches contradictions: "Slide 12 says X but Slide 13 says Y — how do I reconcile?" He also checks deck numbers against daily reports, prior meetings, and verbal statements.

### 3. "Others" Questions Come From What's MISSING
A large portion of Ian's questions (typically 20-30%) are in the "Others" section and ask about topics the deck DOESN'T cover — prior meeting follow-ups, competitive program updates, adjacent topics he tracks.

### 4. Operating Model Mechanics, Not Just Outcomes
For any new initiative, Ian digs into HOW it works: "How do MCN earn from us? Lump sum or commission?" / "Where will the leads for call centres come from?" / "How many people, how long, org chart fit?" This goes beyond "financial discipline" into end-to-end operating model scrutiny.

### 5. Questions Are Concrete, Not Abstract
Ian asks "CPO of Anbo is so much higher than NNWH?" (referencing a specific table cell) — not "What is the cost comparison between warehouses?" Every question is anchored to something specific on the slide.

### 6. Stakeholder Calibration
Ian frequently tags specific people and asks them to verify or align: "@Yuna is the impact aligned with your thinking?" / "Has this been aligned with @Shuning and @Ning Li?" He also challenges prior statements: "Feels lower than what you were saying."

### 7. Slide Numbers Are Approximate
Ian's slide references are often off by 1-2 from PDF page numbers due to cover pages, section dividers, or pre-read numbering. Content-based mapping is more reliable than page-number matching.
